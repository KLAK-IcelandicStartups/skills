# JTBD Case Studies

## 1. McDonald's Milkshake

### Situation
McDonald's wanted to increase milkshake sales. Traditional research (focus groups, surveys) gave conflicting results: "less sweet," "more chocolate," "more fruit chunks."

### Job Discovery
Observation revealed that 40% of milkshakes were bought in the morning, by solo drivers, to-go.

**Morning job:** "When I'm driving alone to work and have a boring hour of commute, I want something to keep my hands busy, satisfy my hunger until lunch, and give me a small pleasure."

**Afternoon job (different!):** "When picking up my child from school, I want to be a good parent and give them a small reward, but without feeling guilty."

### Competition Through Job Lens
- Morning job: banana, bagel, coffee, boredom, podcast
- Afternoon job: ice cream, candy, toy, parental attention

### Solution
- **Morning milkshake:** thicker (takes longer to drink), with chunks (more interesting), larger, easier to buy (drive-through kiosk)
- **Afternoon milkshake:** smaller (sense of moderation), faster to drink (child doesn't get bored)

### Lesson
The same product can serve completely different jobs. Improving the "product" without understanding the job leads nowhere.

## 2. Southern New Hampshire University (SNHU)

### Situation
SNHU was a small college with 2,500 students. They launched an online program that exploded to 100,000+ students.

### Job Discovery
Typical online student: 30+ years old, working, often with family, interrupted education years ago.

**Job:** "When I realize I'm stuck in my career and need a degree to advance, but I have a job, family, and little time, I want to get a degree without turning my life upside down."

### Job Dimensions
- **Functional:** Get a degree from an accredited institution
- **Emotional:** Feel that I'm growing, prove to myself that I can do it
- **Social:** Show my family I'm setting a good example; not look like a "student" at work

### Competition
- Doing nothing (most common!)
- Other online universities
- Industry certifications
- Changing jobs without a degree

### Breakthrough Changes
1. **Onboarding:** Calling applicants within minutes (not days) - reduces "can I do this" anxiety
2. **Personal advisor:** One point of contact throughout studies - like GPS, not bureaucracy
3. **Credit transfer:** Liberal policy for recognizing previous courses - faster to the goal
4. **Communication:** Simplicity, no academic jargon - "we work for you, not the other way around"

### Lesson
SNHU doesn't compete with Harvard - it competes with "doing nothing." The job isn't "education" but "unfreezing a stuck life."

## 3. American Girl

### Situation
Premium doll company ($100+) at a time when competitors offered dolls for $20.

### Job
**Mothers/grandmothers:** "When I want to have a meaningful moment with my daughter/granddaughter and pass on values, I want an experience that connects us and teaches, not just a toy."

**Girls (8-12):** "When I'm at an age where I don't want to be a 'little girl' anymore but I'm not yet a teenager, I want something that takes me seriously and says my interests matter."

### Dimensions
- **Functional:** Playing with doll, reading stories
- **Emotional:** Feeling "grown up," understood
- **Social:** Ritual with mother/grandmother, belonging to American Girl "club"

### Experience Integration
- Doll + book with story (context, values)
- Stores with hair salons for dolls
- Restaurants where girls eat with their dolls
- Magazine, events, community

### Competition
- Not other dolls (!)
- Other "moments with daughter": trips, movies, shopping
- Other ways to pass on values: conversations, books
- Non-consumption: "I'll just buy something at the store"

### Lesson
American Girl sells an experience-ritual, not a doll. That's why $100+ price makes sense - it's not a "toy" but a "moment with daughter."

## 4. Intuit QuickBooks

### Situation
QuickBooks dominated among small businesses. Attempts to enter the "even smaller" segment (freelancers, single-person) by simplifying the product didn't work.

### Job Discovery
Freelancers have a different job than small businesses.

**Small business job:** "Manage company finances professionally and in compliance with regulations"

**Freelancer job:** "When I send an invoice to a client, I want to know I'll get paid, and I don't want to feel like an amateur"

### Breakthrough: Mint.com → QuickBooks Self-Employed
Instead of "stripped-down QuickBooks" - a completely new product:
- Automatic expense tracking (not manual bookkeeping)
- Bank account integration
- Simple split: business vs personal
- Real-time tax estimation

### Lesson
"Simpler version of the same thing" isn't innovation. Different job requires different product, not a simplified old one.

## 5. Airbnb vs Hotels

### Business traveler job
"When I travel for work, I want efficiency, predictability, and ability to work"
→ Hotel wins (standardization, wifi, desk, room service)

### Family traveler job
"When we go somewhere with kids for a week, we want to feel at home, have space, cook, be together"
→ Airbnb wins (kitchen, rooms, locality, group pricing)

### "Experience" job
"When I want to really get to know a place and feel the locality, not be a tourist"
→ Airbnb wins (local hosts, authentic locations)

### Lesson
Airbnb doesn't compete with "hotels" - it competes for specific jobs. For some jobs, a hotel is a better "employee."

## 6. HOPP (Iceland) — E-Scooter Rental

### Situation
Reykjavík was being ignored by global micromobility companies like Lime and Bird — the city was seen as too small, too windy, too weather-challenged. A team of Icelandic software developers founded HOPP in 2019 rather than waiting for someone else to show up.

### Job Discovery
HOPP's users aren't "renting scooters." They're solving a specific urban mobility gap.

**The job:** "When I need to get across town and it's too far to walk but too short and expensive for a taxi, I want a quick, cheap, effortless way to move, so I can get where I'm going without wasting time or money."

### Job Dimensions
- **Functional:** Get from A to B faster than walking, cheaper than a taxi
- **Emotional:** Feel independent and spontaneous — "I can just go"
- **Social:** Be seen as environmentally conscious; part of modern city life

### Competition Through Job Lens
- Walking (free, but slow)
- Taxis (reliable, but expensive for short trips)
- Private car (convenient, but parking is painful and expensive)
- Bus (cheap, but infrequent and slow for short trips)
- Doing nothing — just not going, or delaying the trip
- Other scooter companies (Bolt entered Reykjavík in 2024 with 800 scooters)

### What Made HOPP Win

1. **Local-first franchise model:** Instead of operating everything from HQ, HOPP franchises to local entrepreneurs. Local owners know the city, care about the community, and provide better service. This is a direct response to the "anxiety" force — people trust local operators more than faceless corporations.

2. **Software-first approach:** The founders were developers, not logistics people. They automated demand forecasting, staff scheduling, and operations — making it viable to run in small cities where Bird and Lime couldn't justify the overhead.

3. **Multi-modal pivot:** When they noticed users also needed rides in bad weather or for longer distances, HOPP added taxi booking to the same app (2024). They followed the job, not the product category.

4. **Underserved markets:** Rather than fighting for London or Paris, HOPP targeted cities that global players ignored — Reykjavík, Faroe Islands, small Greek islands, Costa Blanca. Less competition, grateful customers.

### Forces at Play
- **Push:** Taxis are expensive, walking is slow, parking is painful
- **Pull:** Instant availability via app, affordable per-minute pricing, green credentials
- **Anxiety:** "Will there be a scooter near me?" "Is it safe in Reykjavík weather?" → Addressed by reliable fleet management and local presence
- **Habit:** Walking or driving → Reduced by making scooters available everywhere and the app dead simple

### Lesson
HOPP didn't build a better scooter — they built a better way to serve the "get across town" job in cities that everyone else ignored. The franchise model and software-first approach let them operate profitably where competitors couldn't, and following the job (not the product) led them to add taxis when the circumstances demanded it. Now operating in 15+ countries.

## 7. Krónan Snjallverslun (Iceland) — Smart Grocery Shopping

### Situation
Krónan, Iceland's second-largest grocery chain (28% market share), faced the same problem every supermarket faces: grocery shopping is a chore. Long checkout lines, forgotten items, the mental load of meal planning. In a small country with harsh weather and busy households, every wasted minute at the store is a minute people resent.

### Job Discovery
Krónan looked beyond "sell groceries" and asked what job the shopping experience itself needed to do.

**The job:** "When I need to feed my household and I'm short on time, I want to get exactly what I need with as little friction as possible, so I can spend my time on things that actually matter to me."

### Job Dimensions
- **Functional:** Acquire the right groceries efficiently
- **Emotional:** Feel in control, not stressed — "I've got this handled"
- **Social:** Be seen as an organized, capable parent/partner who has dinner sorted

### The Snjallverslun Solution

Krónan didn't build one feature — they built a system that addresses the job across multiple circumstances:

| Circumstance | Solution | Job served |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| "I'm at the store and hate waiting in line" | **Scan & Go** — scan items with your phone, pay in-app, walk out | Eliminate checkout friction |
| "I'm home and don't want to go to the store" | **Online ordering + delivery** to 96 postcodes across Iceland | Shop without leaving home |
| "I don't know what to cook" | **Recipe integration** — browse recipes, add all ingredients to cart with one tap | Solve meal planning |
| "I buy the same things every week" | **One-click reorder** of previous purchases | Remove repetitive decisions |
| "My partner and I keep buying duplicates" | **Shared lists and group shopping** for families and workplaces | Coordinate household purchasing |

### Competition Through Job Lens
- **Other supermarkets** (Bónus, Hagkaup, Nettó) — obvious, but they compete on price, not experience
- **Meal kit delivery services** — solve the "what to cook" job
- **Restaurant delivery / takeout** — solve "I don't want to cook at all"
- **Fast food** — the "I need food now, zero effort" job
- **Doing nothing** — skipping a proper meal, eating whatever's in the fridge

### What Made It Work

1. **Multiple modes, one job:** Snjallverslun isn't just an app — it's online ordering, Scan & Go, recipe integration, and shared lists all serving the same core job. Customers choose the mode that fits their circumstance.

2. **Eliminated the worst friction first:** Checkout lines are the single most hated part of grocery shopping. Scan & Go killed them entirely. Krónan's goal of 40% self-checkout adoption was "reached and surpassed from day one."

3. **Built for repeat behavior (Little Hire):** One-click reordering, recipe-to-cart, and shared lists aren't flashy features — they're designed to make the Little Hire effortless. Every weekly shop is easier than the last.

4. **Local technology partnership:** Built by Reon, an Icelandic dev shop, using Scanbot SDK for reliable barcode scanning. The tech was designed for Iceland's specific retail environment, not adapted from a generic global solution.

### Forces at Play
- **Push:** Checkout lines waste time, meal planning is exhausting, forgetting items means a second trip
- **Pull:** Scan & Go is faster, recipes solve "what's for dinner," reordering is effortless
- **Anxiety:** "Will the scanner work?" "Will my delivery be right?" → Addressed by intuitive UX, clear visual feedback, and reliable fulfillment
- **Habit:** "I've always just walked the aisles and stood in line" → Reduced by making the app so frictionless that the old way feels slow by comparison

### Lesson
Krónan didn't try to be the cheapest grocery store (Bónus and Prís own that job). Instead, they asked: "What if the shopping experience itself was the product?" Snjallverslun turned grocery shopping from a chore into a system — and 50% year-over-year growth proved that Icelandic families were desperate for someone to solve the "feed my household without the hassle" job.

## Patterns from Case Studies

### 1. Same product, different jobs
Morning vs afternoon milkshake - require different solutions even though it's "the same product."

### 2. Competition from unexpected places
- SNHU: competitor is "doing nothing"
- American Girl: competitor is "other activities with daughter"
- Milkshake: competitor is banana and boredom
- HOPP: competitor is walking, taxis, and just not going
- Krónan: competitor is takeout, meal kits, and eating whatever's in the fridge

### 3. Integration for poorly understood jobs
American Girl and SNHU heavily integrated the experience because the job was poorly served by existing solutions. Krónan did the same — combining ordering, scanning, recipes, and shared lists into one system because no single feature solved the whole job.

### 4. Emotions and social aspects often more important than functionality
- SNHU: "prove to myself I can do it"
- American Girl: "moment with daughter"
- Milkshake: "small pleasure to start the day"
- HOPP: "feel independent and spontaneous"
- Krónan: "feel in control, not stressed about dinner"

### 5. Anti-change forces are powerful
SNHU won mainly by reducing anxiety (quick callback, personal advisor) - not by "better education."

### 6. Follow the job, not the product category
HOPP started with scooters but added taxis when they realized the job was "get across town," not "ride a scooter." Krónan built recipes and shared lists because the job was "feed my household efficiently," not "buy groceries."

### 7. Underserved markets are goldmines
HOPP targeted cities that global players ignored. Krónan focused on making the shopping experience the differentiator, not price. Both found opportunity where others saw markets too small or too competitive to bother with.
