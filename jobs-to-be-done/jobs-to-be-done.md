# Jobs to Be Done for Startups

Help founders discover and articulate what "job" customers hire their product to do, then turn that insight into concrete strategy.

## How to Use This Skill

This skill is interactive. Don't lecture — guide the founder through the framework by understanding their situation first, then producing the right deliverable.

### Step 1: Understand the Context

Start by figuring out what the founder actually needs. Ask something like:

> "What are you working on? Tell me about your product and where you are right now — are you trying to figure out your value proposition, preparing for customer interviews, analyzing why people churn, or something else?"

Based on their answer, take the appropriate path:

| Situation | What to produce |
|-----------|-----------------|
| "I need to articulate what we do" | Job Statement + Job Canvas |
| "I want to understand our customers better" | Interview Script + guidance |
| "People sign up but don't stick around" | Forces Worksheet + churn analysis |
| "I need to figure out our competition" | Competitive Job Map |
| "I'm building my pitch / value prop" | Job Statement + Positioning Statement |
| "I want to run a JTBD workshop" | Full walkthrough: Job Statement → Forces → Canvas |
| General / unclear | Start with the Job Statement, then expand |

### Step 2: Guide, Don't Dump

Work through the framework one piece at a time. After each step, check in with the founder before moving on. A good rhythm:

1. **Ask** about their customers and circumstances
2. **Draft** the relevant artifact (job statement, canvas, etc.)
3. **Review** it together — ask if it rings true
4. **Refine** based on their feedback
5. **Move on** to the next piece when they're ready

## Core Concept

Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in their lives. A **Job to Be Done** is the progress a customer wants to make in a specific circumstance.

Three things make this powerful for startups:

- **Progress, not features.** Customers care about moving from where they are to where they want to be. Features are just the means.
- **Circumstances, not demographics.** The same person has different jobs in different situations. "30-year-old male" tells you nothing; "founder who just got their first paying customer and needs to figure out invoicing" tells you everything.
- **Hiring and firing.** Customers actively choose ("hire") a product for a job, and they'll drop ("fire") it when something else does the job better — or when the job changes.

## The Three Dimensions

Every job has three dimensions. Startups that only address the functional dimension build commodities. The ones that nail all three build products people love.

| Dimension | Question | Startup example |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Functional** | What does the customer need to get done? | "Send invoices and get paid" |
| **Emotional** | How do they want to feel? | "Feel like a real business, not winging it" |
| **Social** | How do they want to be perceived? | "Look professional to clients" |

## Practical Templates

### Template 1: Job Statement

The foundation of everything else. Use this format:

```
When [circumstances],
I want to [progress],
so I can [outcome].
```

**How to write a good one:**
- Never mention your product or any specific solution
- Include the circumstance — it's what makes the job specific and actionable
- Cover functional, emotional, and social needs
- It should be specific enough to build for, broad enough to allow creative solutions

**Startup examples:**

> **Fintech for freelancers:**
> "When I finish a project for a client and need to get paid, I want to send a professional invoice quickly and track whether it's been paid, so I can focus on my work instead of chasing money and feel like I'm running a real business."

> **Team collaboration tool:**
> "When my remote team is working on a project and things start falling through the cracks, I want to see what everyone's doing and what's blocked, so I can keep us on track without micromanaging or scheduling another meeting."

> **Marketplace for local services:**
> "When something breaks in my apartment and I don't know how to fix it, I want to find a reliable person quickly without overpaying, so I can get it sorted and stop worrying about it."

> **E-scooter rental (HOPP):**
> "When I need to get across town and it's too far to walk but too short and expensive for a taxi, I want a quick, cheap, effortless way to move, so I can get where I'm going without wasting time or money."

> **Smart grocery shopping (Krónan Snjallverslun):**
> "When I need to feed my household and I'm short on time, I want to get exactly what I need with as little friction as possible, so I can spend my time on things that actually matter to me."

**Litmus test** — a good job statement should:
1. Make sense without knowing what your product is
2. Include a specific circumstance (not just "when I need to...")
3. Touch on how the customer wants to feel, not just what they need done
4. Be something a real person would nod along to

### Template 2: Forces of Progress Worksheet

Four forces determine whether a customer will switch to your product. Change only happens when Push + Pull > Habit + Anxiety.

```
FORCES OF PROGRESS — [Product Name]

═══ FORCES FOR CHANGE ═══

PUSH (frustration with current situation):
• [What's broken or painful about how they do it today?]
• [What triggered them to start looking?]
• [What's getting worse over time?]

PULL (attraction of your solution):
• [What does your product promise that excites them?]
• [What would their life look like after switching?]
• [What specific moment would feel better?]

═══ FORCES AGAINST CHANGE ═══

ANXIETY (fear about your solution):
• [What could go wrong if they switch?]
• [What don't they know or understand about your product?]
• [What's the worst-case scenario in their mind?]

HABIT (comfort with current behavior):
• [What are they used to doing today?]
• [What would they have to learn or change?]
• [Who else would be affected by switching?]

═══ STRATEGY ═══

How we increase Push + Pull:
• [specific actions]

How we reduce Anxiety + Habit:
• [specific actions — often more impactful than increasing Pull]
```

**Key insight for startups:** Most founders instinctively focus on making their product more attractive (Pull). But often the bigger wins come from reducing Anxiety (free trials, migration help, social proof) and breaking Habit (making the switch effortless). This is especially true for early-stage startups where trust is low.

### Template 3: Customer Discovery Interview Script

Based on the "timeline" method — reconstructing the customer's decision journey from first thought to purchase. This reveals the actual job, not what they think they want.

**Who to interview:** Recent customers (within 1-2 weeks of signing up or purchasing). 30-45 minutes. Record with permission.

**Opening:**
> "I'd love to hear about how you ended up using [product]. I'm not trying to sell you anything — I just want to understand your experience."

**The Timeline:**

| Phase | Questions | What you're looking for |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|
| **First thought** | "When did you first realize you needed something like this? What was going on?" | The circumstance and push |
| **Passive looking** | "What did you do after that? Did you look at alternatives? What stopped you from acting?" | Habit and anxiety forces |
| **Active search** | "What made you start seriously looking? What changed?" | The tipping point |
| **Decision** | "Walk me through when you decided. What convinced you? What almost stopped you?" | Pull and remaining anxiety |
| **Experience** | "Is it doing what you expected? What surprised you? What's still missing?" | Job completion, Big Hire vs Little Hire |

**Closing:**
> "If you could change one thing about your experience — from finding us to using us — what would it be?"

**After the interview:** Extract the job statement, forces, and any workarounds or non-obvious competitors they mentioned. Five to ten interviews usually reveal clear patterns.

## Key Concepts (Quick Reference)

### Big Hire vs Little Hire

Two distinct decisions: the **Big Hire** (signing up / purchasing — happens once) and the **Little Hire** (choosing to use it in the moment — happens repeatedly). Many startups win the Big Hire with great marketing but lose the Little Hire because daily usage is clunky or doesn't deliver. If retention is a problem, the issue is almost always the Little Hire.

### Non-Obvious Competition

Your real competition isn't just other startups in your category. It's everything a customer could "hire" for the same job — including spreadsheets, manual processes, asking a friend, or doing nothing at all. "Doing nothing" is often the biggest competitor for early-stage startups.

**Example:** A startup building a project management tool doesn't just compete with Asana and Monday. They compete with spreadsheets, Slack threads, sticky notes, email, regular standup meetings, and one person keeping everything in their head.

### Positioning Through the Job Lens

```
For [customers in these circumstances]
who [have this job to do],
[product] is the [frame of reference]
that [does the job better because],
unlike [alternative they're using now],
we [key differentiator on the job].
```

**Example:**
> "For early-stage founders who are drowning in admin work and just want to focus on building, Pilot is the bookkeeping service that handles your finances so you never think about them, unlike doing it yourself in spreadsheets or hiring a local accountant you have to manage."

## Common Startup Mistakes

These come up repeatedly when working with founders:

- **Describing the product through features instead of the job.** "We're an AI-powered analytics platform with real-time dashboards" vs "We help growth teams figure out what's actually driving signups so they stop guessing."
- **Defining competition too narrowly.** Looking only at other startups in your category and missing that most of your potential customers are currently using spreadsheets, email, or nothing at all.
- **Only investing in Pull.** Making the product shinier instead of reducing the fear and friction of switching. Early adopters need trust signals, easy onboarding, and low commitment.
- **Winning signups but losing daily usage.** The Big Hire / Little Hire gap. If people sign up but don't come back, the product isn't doing the daily job well enough.
- **Segmenting by demographics.** "Our target is tech-savvy millennials" doesn't help you build anything. "People who just started freelancing and are terrified of messing up their taxes" does.
